It's been said that Hunter S. Thompson typed out *The Great Gatsby* as an
exercise, sometimes you hear he did it over and over again even. I have no idea
if it's true or in the case that it's not true I don't know if he was even the
source of the story, but in any case the theory makes sense. The story goes that
he just wanted to feel what it would be like to write the great american novel.
Typing is in the fingers not the head. Whether it's true or not I've been doing
the same thing with AI drafts and it seems to work. Claude (other language
models are available) is a pretty decent vomit drafter but no matter how you
prompt it the output *sounds like* AI to me. Furthermore it's very hard to
force yourself to **read** every. single. word. of the output to make *sure* it
says what you think it says and not more or less, but when you type it all back
out again it's impossible not to.

I do not copy or paste anything (except code examples), I do not edit. I retype
the whole thing, and as I do I end up re-writing a LOT of it.

This probably sounds inefficient. It is, but that's the point. You can probably
stop reading here, I think I've made the point, but I'm going to keep blathering
on for a while longer.

---

The obvious question is why does editing not work as well. Read a draft, fix the
awkward parts, delete filler, add missing information, done. It's the process
you'd use if you had written the first draft yourself; it's probably what your
high-school English teacher told you to do. For me, it doesn't work.

Editing is an analytical process. You're looking for problems, you're scanning
the text, pattern-matching against a mental list of things that "look wrong".
Maybe passive voice, run-on sentences, unintended repetition. You find one, fix
it, go back to scanning.

The issue with this is that anything that's "between" problems gets a free pass.
If your error detector doesn't go off you skip past it. You're reading at idea
speed and not individual word speed. Writing forces you to see what the writing
*says* and not what it *means*. Retyping forces you to work at word speed. You
can't really skip things because you have to press the actual keys. When you
type a word you'll feel a much lower threshold of wrongness, at least that's what
happens to me.

---

Sometimes you're writing whole new sentences but when you run out of inspiration
you can just retype the AI draft and if it feels right to you you keep it. I
find that sometimes I just stop typing, something in my mind registers vague
wrongness, and then it's a relatively simple matter of rephrasing or skipping
over the phrase. Some posts end up looking not much (or nothing) like the AI
draft, some are relatively closer.

Anyway, the AI draft of this post was about twice this length but the rest is
just boring restatements of what I've already said here. I'm not entirely sure
I would have noticed that if I'd tried to just edit it.
